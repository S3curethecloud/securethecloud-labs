📖 Concept Deep Dives

1. What is evidence capture for AI red-team findings?

Evidence capture is the structured documentation of what was reviewed, what control was expected, what behavior was observed, what uncertainty remains, and what remediation is recommended. The goal is to create a reviewer-safe, reproducible finding.

2. Why must findings separate observation from conclusion?

Observations describe what the evidence shows — facts, logs, behaviors, or artifacts. Conclusions explain what the observation means — risk, impact, or required action.

🔍 Observation vs. Conclusion Examples

  • Observation: "The AI workflow executed a tool call without an explicit approval record."
  • Conclusion: "This suggests the approval gate may be bypassed when the model is confident."
  • Observation: "Retrieved content from an untrusted source influenced the model's recommendation."
  • Conclusion: "The system lacks source authority ranking, creating prompt injection risk."

Keeping them separate helps reviewers distinguish facts, assumptions, risk interpretation, and remediation judgment.

3. What belongs in reviewer-safe evidence?

Reviewer-safe evidence should include:

🎯 Objective: What was being evaluated?
📏 Scope: What was in/out of bounds?
🔧 Preconditions: What was assumed or set up?
🛡️ Expected control: What should have prevented the issue?
🧪 Synthetic artifacts: Non-production evidence of behavior
👁️ Observed behavior: What actually happened?
⏱️ Timestamps/references: When available
❓ Uncertainty: What wasn't tested or proven?
🔧 Remediation guidance: What should be fixed?
4. How should uncertainty and limits be recorded?

Uncertainty should clearly state what was not tested, what evidence was synthetic, which assumptions were made, and what the finding cannot prove.

❓ Examples of Good Uncertainty Statements

  • "This finding is based on a synthetic test case. Real-world behavior may vary based on model version and prompt tuning."
  • "The review did not test tenant isolation boundaries. Future testing should verify cross-tenant access is blocked."
  • "This observation does not prove compromise. It identifies a missing control that could increase risk in production."
  • "The finding does not account for recent model updates or custom safety training."
5. How should remediation be tied to evidence?

Remediation should map directly to the observed behavior and expected control. A strong finding avoids vague advice and explains which boundary, control, or review process should change.

🔧 Remediation Mapping Examples

  • Observation: Model bypassed approval gate → Remediation: Add explicit approval-state validation before tool execution
  • Observation: Retrieved content overrode instructions → Remediation: Implement source authority ranking and context labeling
  • Observation: No evidence of logging → Remediation: Add structured logging for all policy decisions
6. What makes an AI red-team finding portfolio-ready?

A portfolio-ready finding is:

  • 📏 Scoped — Clear boundaries of what was tested
  • 🛡️ Safe — No sensitive data, credentials, or exploit output
  • 🔄 Repeatable — Another reviewer could reproduce the reasoning
  • 📖 Reviewer-readable — Clear language, no hidden assumptions
  • ❓ Explicit about unknowns — What was not proven
  • 🎯 Evidence-backed — Claims tied to synthetic artifacts
  • 🚫 Not overstating compromise — Risk-focused, not alarmist

⚠️ High-Risk Anti-Pattern

A dangerous pattern is treating raw exploit output, sensitive data, screenshots with secrets, or unsupported claims as proof of an AI red-team finding.

❌ Unsafe Pattern

  • Raw sensitive evidence
  • Real secrets or tokens
  • Customer records
  • Exploit output copied into a report
  • Unclear scope
  • Unsupported compromise claims
  • Missing uncertainty

✅ Safe Alternative

  • Synthetic evidence artifact
  • Scoped objective
  • Expected control
  • Observed behavior
  • Explicit uncertainty
  • Remediation mapped to evidence
  • Reviewer-safe conclusion
